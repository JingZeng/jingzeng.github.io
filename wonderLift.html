WONDER LIFT

DESIGN BRIEF

We were challenged to design and prototype a digital technology or application that augment an existing “dumb” infrastructure within cities. The product needs to be interactive and playful, and inspired by a 1950’s or 1960’s aesthetic. 


WE FOCUS ON THE ”DUMB” ELEVATOR

  • How can we design interactive elevator to give users a fun and playful experience?
  • How can we find a way to kill time amongst users waiting for the lift?
  • How can we encourage people not to rely on their mobile devices to avoid boredom?

OUR SOLUTION

Multiple users from inside and outside the elevator can collaborate and contribute together into the playfulness of the concept. The Whack-A-Mole game is added as a never-ending activity which allows users to have a run through of the entire concept while waiting for the lift.

DESIGN TEAM

This was a team of four students. My primary role was Programmer and secondary role was UX Designer.

Initial Research

Alice in wonderland movie

It was a struggle to fit in the 1950/60's aesthetics in the beginning until we realised we could choose a movie theme. We carried out an online questionnaire which resulted in 92 people contributing to it - the result showed that the majority of participants would want to see an animation film as a theme in an elevator.

Psychedelic art

Wonderland theme into our design, we did some research on 1950/60’s aesthetics. We were interested in Psychedelic Art at that time.

We created several psychedelic images inspired by an Alice in Wonderland movie poster to use as our interface background.

Making pressure plates

We did some research and found YouTube tutorials on how to make a pressure plate. We prepared materials required to make this feasible, including sponges, aluminium foil, wires, tracing papers, cardboards and masking tapes.

Design Process - Iterations

First Prototype

In the first prototype, we placed a wire in between two aluminium foil, on each cardboards (we need 1 for top and bottom of the pressure plate), then had two sponges with a hole in the middle to allow top and bottom aluminium foils to meet when pressed.


At first we had just single LEDs to test on. We linked them into series and then into the Arduino. To have our characters light up together with the LEDs, we laser printed a 20x20cm acrylic sheet and stuck a tracing paper in hope that all the light would diffuse throughout the entire pressure plate.


Theoretically, when player stands on the pressure plate, two aluminium foils should get in touch, and the LED lights will automatically light up, and if she/he lift his/her feet, LED should be turned off.


Single LEDs lighting effects
Second Prototype

In the previous prototype stage we cut out a small 20x20cm square from a recycled cardboard material to act as our base. This was highly durable and we relied on this for the pressure plates we made afterwards. We also cut out different sized circles on a new sponge to increase the sensitivity level.


We also switched our Single LEDs to an LED Strip as it was more vibrant in colour and improve the aesthetics.


As part of our design brief, at this stage we created a simple Whack-A-Mole game interface in Processing. We gathered some images of the villains in the Alice in Wonderland movie, and arranged them into a 3 by 3 square size. These characters would have a tint value at a random interval and when a user taps on them their tint value increases, making it look like they're disappearing. Also for extra reactiveness - these characters will produce a shrieking sound when you tap on them, as if they've been defeated.


LED Strip and Tablet Interface
Third Prototype

We started off by cutting our new mat to a 35x35cm square size. We provided ourselves with around 45 meters of wires to link up a total of nine mats with the Arduino board and external batteries to save our Arduino from frying out.


Based on the user testing in the previous prototype, users wanted a bigger tablet interface so we switched to a 13 inch foldable laptop screen that allows touch screen.


We also created several psychedelic background images inspired by an Alice in Wonderland movie poster using photoshop.


New Pressure Plate and Tablet Interface
Final Prototype

We decorated a virtual elevator by adding walls, buttons and installed a web camera inside. Based on the findings from the last evaluation. We printed out an instruction poster inside the lift to show rules on how to play. We justified the position of video capture on the screen and rotated it to 90 degrees by changing the code. Finally, we gathered all the wires and batteries into a box and hid it from users on the other side of the wall.


An interactive “Wonderland lift” is now published !

Developing the Code

Initially, the Arduino and Processing codes worked independently. While the Whack-a-mole game was fully functional on the tablet, the interactive floor only had two semi functioning pressure plates. I had to replace our pressure plates at times with push buttons to test the double-tap function on the LED strips.


When I figured out the problem with the pressure plate not working properly, I attempted to connect Arduino to Processing. The idea was to have the game run in Processing, while through a serial communication the Arduino would read the random states of each pressure plate, then decides the LED to turn on or off.


In order to fix a few bugs at this stage, I went back to improve the game code on Processing, so that the randomness signals would be smoother when communicating through serial. Lastly, I connected the tablet with pressure plates. Through trial and errors I decided to use two Arduino ports- one connecting 5 LED strips and pressure mats and the other connecting the remaining 4 sets.


Later I programmed different colours and added sound effects to each characters in the interface. The sounds will come out through a speaker connected by Bluetooth.
